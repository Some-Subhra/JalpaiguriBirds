Rarity Read - The lesser-known denizens

Hooded PittaPitta sordida

Hooded Pitta (Male)

The Bird


English Synonyms

Green-breasted Pitta,   Jewel Thrush,   Black-headed Pitta (novaeguineae)

Taxonomy

Order :   PASSERIFORMES

Family :   Pittidae

Genus :   Pitta

Species :  P. sordida

Size , Weight & Diet

Length of 16 to 19 cm and a weight of 42 to 70 g.

Diet consists of various insects (including their larvae), which they hunt on the ground, and berries.


Behaviour & Habitat


North Sikkim is one of the best birding destinations for someone looking for high altitude species in the Eastern Himalayas. As the road from Gangtok to Chungthang generally remains a bit heavy with traffic, serious birding can start only after Chungthang.

Hooded Pitta in it's Habitat


Morning birding around the village of Lachung will yield species like Black-faced Laughinhthrush, White Collared Blackbird, Dark-breasted Rosefinch, Grey-backed Shrike, Spotted Nutcracker,Yellow-billed Blue Magpie etc. Further towards Yumthang in the Rhodo forest, birds like Whistler’s Warber,Hume’s Warbler, Rufous-vented Tit, Coal Tit, Fire-tailed Sunbird, Rufous-gorgetedFlycatcher, Red-headed Bullfinch etc. are not uncommon. The Yumthang valley is worth looking for breeding Ibisbill& Broad-billed Warblers. Red-billed Choughs are more common here than their Yellow-billed cousins. With luck on your side you may see your first Grandala which sometimes flock in numbers more than 50.

Nest with chicks

On the otherside of the ridge, the Lachen Valley is more forested. The Bamboo brakes just at the entrance of the village is a sure shot area for Brown Parrotbills. Lachen is also one of the best sites to see the Speckled Wood Pigeon apart from the other commoners found in the Lachung Valley. Flocks of Scarlet Finch have also been observed here during July & August. The habitat between Lachen&Thangu has got a bit disturbed by the ongoing road construction works. Previously Blood Pheasant, Red Crossbill, Snow Pigeonetc. have been observed along this stretch from time to time. Along the road to Gurudrongmar from Thangu lookout for species like Ibisbill, Hume’s Groundpecker, Streaked Rosefinch, Snow Pigeon, Red-billed Chough, Horned Lark etc. The rare White-rumped Snowfinch is best looked for around the Pika burrows. The area around Gurudrongmar is particularly good for White-throated Dipper ( both races), Hume Short-toed Lark, Horned Lark, Brandt’s Mountain Finch, Plain Mountain Finch, the rare Rufous-necked Snowfinch, Robin Accentor etc. This is also good site for the White-winged Redstart. Ruddy-shelduck has been observed to breed here.

The Mammal life here is also very interesting. Keep your eyes open for species like the Tibetan Wild Ass, Mountain Weasel, Argali, Tibetan Gazelle, Red Fox, Pallas Cat, Himalayan Marmot, Royle’sPika and Black-lipped Pika.

Conservation Status (IUCN)


Least Concern

This species has an extremely large range, and hence does not approach the thresholds for Vulnerable under the range size criterion (Extent of Occurrence <20,000 km2 combined with a declining or fluctuating range size, habitat extent/quality, or population size and a small number of locations or severe fragmentation). Despite the fact that the population trend appears to be decreasing, the decline is not believed to be sufficiently rapid to approach the thresholds for Vulnerable under the population trend criterion (>30% decline over ten years or three generations). The population size has not been quantified, but it is not believed to approach the thresholds for Vulnerable under the population size criterion (<10,000 mature individuals with a continuing decline estimated to be >10% in ten years or three generations, or with a specified population structure). For these reasons the species is evaluated as Least Concern.
